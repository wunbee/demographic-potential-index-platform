Project Overview

What is DPI?

The Demographic Potential Index (DPI) is an advanced analytical framework designed to quantify and evaluate a nation's comprehensive demographic resilience and development capacity across varying historical epochs.

Traditional metrics often look at population growth or fertility rates in isolation. DPI was created to solve this fragmentation by synthesizing critical structural shifts — such as mortality transitions, urbanization rates, educational attainment, and reproductive awareness — into a single, unified index ($D$).

📊 DPI Evaluation Overview

The Demographic Potential Index (DPI) is a comprehensive composite metric designed to evaluate the structural strength, resilience, and sustainability of a demographic system. By quantitative tracking of multi-layered socioeconomic factors, the model moves beyond simple fertility statistics to identify long-term generational reproduction trends.

Each foundational indicator is weighted on a normalized scale, reflecting its evolutionary stage during historical transitions. The final aggregated scoring allows researchers and analysts to categorize any historical period into distinct demographic states, ranging from institutional deadlock up to maximum traditional or balanced sustainable reproduction potential.

🛡️ 1. S — Survivance (Survival Index)

Definition: Represents mortality conditions, population survivability, and the overall level of public health.
Measurement Basis: Crude Death Rate (CDR), deaths per 1,000 population.
Score CDR (per 1,000) Demographic Condition
5.0 0.0 – 9.0 Optimal — Advanced healthcare, high life expectancy, and very low mortality.
4.0 9.1 – 12.5 Stable — Modern healthcare system with relatively low mortality.
3.0 12.6 – 20.0 Moderate — Transitional mortality conditions and improving healthcare.
2.0 20.1 – 50.0 High Mortality — Traditional demographic conditions with elevated mortality.
1.0 >50.0 Critical — Severe mortality caused by war, famine, epidemics, or humanitarian crises.

👨‍👩‍👧 2. T — Tradition (Traditional Fertility Index)

Definition: Represents the strength of traditional fertility behaviour based on the Total Fertility Rate (TFR).
Measurement Basis: Total Fertility Rate (average number of children per woman).
Score TFR Range Characteristic
5.0 ≥ 6.0 Maximum Traditional Potential — Very large families remain the social norm.
4.5 5.5 – 5.9 Very High Traditional Fertility — Large families remain dominant.
4.0 4.6 – 5.4 High Traditional Fertility — Early demographic transition begins.
3.5 3.8 – 4.5 Moderately High Fertility — Transition toward smaller family sizes.
3.0 3.0 – 3.7 Moderate Fertility — Three-child family model predominates.
2.5 2.4 – 2.9 Transitional Fertility — Traditional fertility patterns weaken.
2.0 2.0 – 2.3 Low Fertility — Near replacement level.
1.5 1.7 – 1.9 Very Low Fertility — Sustained below-replacement fertility.
1.0 <1.7 Demographic Deadlock — Extremely low fertility threatens long-term population replacement.

🧠 3. A — Awareness

Definition: Represents reproductive awareness, family planning, reproductive health knowledge, and individual decision-making regarding childbirth.
Score Stage Interpretation
5.0 Traditional Reproductive Model Childbearing is mainly determined by tradition and social norms. Family planning is minimal.
4.5 Early Reproductive Awareness Basic maternal health knowledge begins to spread.
4.0 Family Planning Development Access to reproductive information becomes widespread.
3.5 High Reproductive Awareness Most families consciously plan the number and timing of children.
3.0 Modern Reproductive Model Childbearing decisions are primarily based on education, career, and personal choice.
2.5 Individualized Family Planning Personal goals increasingly take priority over fertility.
2.0 Post-Traditional Reproductive Model Parenthood becomes one possible life choice rather than a universal expectation.
1.5 Post-Family Model Family loses its central social role; voluntary childlessness becomes increasingly common.
1.0 Complete Individualization Reproductive decisions are almost entirely individualized, resulting in very low natural reproduction.

METHODOLOGY

Mathematical Evolution

Click on any historical period to explore the underlying formulas and the demographic rationale behind them.

1920

Model I (1920–1954)

▼
D = [ ((S + T + A) / 3) / (1 + (C + WE + U)) ] × O

Why is this model used?

This model represents the early demographic transition, when mortality and traditional fertility were the dominant forces shaping population growth. During this period, fertility control, women's education, and urbanization were still limited, so these factors had only a moderate influence on demographic behaviour. Therefore, the model emphasizes the average contribution of the three core demographic drivers (Survivance, Tradition, and Awareness) while treating limiting factors as secondary influences.

1955

Model II (1955–2007)

▼
D = [ (S + T + A) / (C + WE + U) ] × O

Why is this model used?

This model reflects the middle stage of the demographic transition. Rapid urbanization, expansion of women's education, and widespread access to family planning significantly changed reproductive behaviour. These limiting factors became almost as influential as traditional fertility itself. Consequently, the denominator directly incorporates these structural constraints, making the model more sensitive to modernization processes.

2008

Model III (2008–2026)

▼
D = [ (S + T + A) / (1 + ((C + WE + U) / 3)) ] × O

Why is this model used?

This model represents the modern demographic stage, where most structural transitions have already occurred. Fertility control, education, and urbanization remain important, but their influence has become more stable rather than continuously increasing. Dividing the limiting factors by three reduces their dominance and produces a more balanced assessment of demographic potential under contemporary conditions.

Model Limitations

This model is designed for historical analysis of Kazakhstan. It relies on expert qualitative assessments and does not account for sudden external shocks (e.g., climate or migration crises) unless explicitly modeled. It serves as a complementary analytical tool, not an official forecast.

Mathematical Assumptions

  • Assumption 1: All coefficients are normalized to a common numerical scale before being incorporated into the model.
  • Assumption 2: The coefficient O modifies only the final result because external shocks influence the demographic system as a whole rather than individual components.
  • Assumption 3: Historical periods require different mathematical models because the determinants of demographic development changed substantially throughout the twentieth century.
  • Assumption 4: All coefficients are assumed to contribute independently to the final index.
  • Assumption 5: The index measures relative demographic potential rather than exact future population size.
